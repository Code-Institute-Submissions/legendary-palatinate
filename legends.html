Legends of the Palatinate

Welcome to the Palatinate Hunting Association

Nestled in the heart of Germany's Palatinate Forest, our hunting club hails back to Roman times. Rich in tradition, this is a place where reverence for the wilderness intertwines with the thrill of the chase. The Roman emperors chose our majestic landscapes as their hunting grounds, establishing an annual hunting event that became a testament to courage, skill, and endurance. This event, held in the magical midsummer, has become a rite of passage that has lasted for centuries. Today, the Association is a beacon of camaraderie, a community of individuals bound by a shared love for the wilderness and the excitement of the hunt. We value honor, bravery, and respect for the natural world. Join us, and inscribe your name into the annals of our enduring legacy.

Our Origins

Throughout the ages, our hunting club has been shrouded in mystery, its tales and traditions passed down orally through generations. One such account is a passage from Julius Caesar's 'De Bello Gallico', written around 58-49 BC. :


25. Multaque in Hercyniae silvae genera ferarum nasci constat, quae reliquis in locis visa non sint; ex quibus, quae maxime a reliquis differunt, et quae memoratu digna videntur, haec sunt.


27. Sunt item, quae appellantur alces. Harum est consimilis capris figura et varietas pellium, sed magnitudine paulo antecedunt mutilaeque sunt cornibus et crura sine nodis articulisque habent neque quietis causa procumbunt neque, si quo adflictae casu conciderunt, erigere sese aut sublevare possunt. His sunt arbores pro cubilibus: ad eas se applicant atque ita paulum modo reclinatae quietem capiunt. Quarum ex vestigiis cum est animadversum a venatoribus, quo se recipere consuerint, omnes eo loco aut ab radicibus subruunt aut accidunt arbores, tantum ut summa species earum stantium relinquatur. Huc cum se consuetudine reclinaverunt, infirmas arbores pondere adfligunt atque una ipsae concidunt.Sed do eiusmod tempor incididunt ut labore et dolore magna aliqua.


28. [...] Magna vis eorum est et magna velocitas, neque homini neque ferae quam conspexerunt parcunt. Hos studiose foveis captos interficiunt. Hoc se labore durant adulescentes atque hoc genere venationis exercent, et qui plurimos ex his interfecerunt, relatis in publicum cornibus, quae sint testimonio, magnam ferunt laudem.
Sed adsuescere ad homines et mansuefieri ne parvuli quidem excepti possunt. Amplitudo cornuum et figura et species multum a nostrorum boum cornibus differt. Haec studiose conquisita ab labris argento circumcludunt atque in amplissimis epulis pro poculis utuntur.


25. [...] It is certain that many kinds of wild beast are produced in this forest which have not been seen in other parts; of which the following are such as differ principally from other animals, and appear worthy of being committed to record.


27. There are also animals which are called elcs. The shape of these, and the varied color of their skins, is much like roes, but in size they surpass them a little and are destitute of horns, and have legs without joints and ligatures; nor do they lie down for the purpose of rest, nor, if they have been thrown down by any accident, can they raise or lift themselves up. Trees serve as beds to them; they lean themselves against them, and thus reclining only slightly, they take their rest; when the huntsmen have discovered from the footsteps of these animals whither they are accustomed to betake themselves, they either undermine all the trees at the roots, or cut into them so far that the upper part of the trees may appear to be left standing. When they have leant upon them, according to their habit, they knock down by their weight the unsupported trees, and fall down themselves along with them.


28. [...] Their strength and speed are extraordinary; they spare neither man nor wild beast which they have espied. These the Germans take with much pains in pits and kill them. The young men harden themselves with this exercise, and practice themselves in this kind of hunting, and those who have slain the greatest number of them, having produced the horns in public, to serve as evidence, receive great praise. But not even when taken very young can they be rendered familiar to men and tamed. The size, shape, and appearance of their horns differ much from the horns of our oxen. These they anxiously seek after, and bind at the tips with silver, and use as cups at their most sumptuous entertainments.

The text alludes to a large, mysterious creature living in the Germanic forests, an animal our ancestors revered and pursued. Although the text is open to interpretation, we believe it refers to the noble beast that we still hunt today during our annual event.
